A running list of terms I keep coming back to. I add to this as I run into words that deserve a clean definition. Each entry is short and written for engineers who already know the surrounding territory.

## Bisection bandwidth

Cut the network into two equal halves and add up the bandwidth of every link that crosses the cut. That total is the bisection bandwidth for that cut, and the bisection bandwidth of the network is the smallest such total over all the ways you could split it in half. It is a worst case measure, the capacity available across the tightest cut you can find.

It matters because it tells you how much traffic the two halves of the cluster can exchange at once, which is the question that decides whether an all-to-all or an all-reduce runs at full speed or chokes. A network has full bisection bandwidth when that worst case cut still lets every machine on one side talk to a machine on the other at full line rate at the same time. That is the non-blocking property, and it is the whole point of building a fat tree. When people say a fabric is oversubscribed, they mean its bisection bandwidth is less than full, so under heavy all-to-all traffic some flows have to wait.

## Lattice

In a data center context, a lattice is a regular, repeating interconnect topology where compute elements are arranged in a multidimensional grid and each element connects directly to its nearest neighbors. A 2D mesh, a 3D mesh, and a torus (a mesh with wraparound links so the edges loop back) are all lattices. Google's TPU pods are wired as a 3D torus, which is the lattice shape that shows up most often at large scale.

The reason the lattice matters is that it decides which nodes are close to each other. Talking to a neighbor is one hop and cheap. Talking to a node on the far side of the lattice costs many hops. When you map a distributed job onto the hardware, you want the heavy communication to stay between neighbors so it rides the cheap links.

This is where the lattice connects to balancing compute in distributed systems. Amdahl's Law says your maximum speedup is capped by the fraction of work that behaves as serial, and at the scale of 10,000 to 100,000 accelerators treated as one system, coordination overhead is what grows that serial fraction. Synchronization, cross-node communication, stragglers, and serialization points like parameter updates or pipeline bubbles all act serial even when no single line of code is. A lattice that matches the communication pattern of the job keeps that overhead local and small, so the effective serial fraction stays low and the hardware keeps scaling. A poor mapping forces traffic across the whole lattice, inflates the overhead, grows the serial fraction, and is exactly why more hardware can stop buying you more throughput.

So when people arrange accelerators in a lattice, they are choosing a shape that makes the common collective operations, like an all-reduce, land on short neighbor-to-neighbor paths instead of long ones. The lattice is a tool for shrinking the serial and overhead part that Amdahl's Law punishes you for.

## Optical switch

![Schematic of an optical switch steering light from one fiber to several perpendicular output fibers](/assets/img/glossary/optical-switch-mirror.webp)
_A mirror steers the incoming light to one of several output fibers. Tilt the mirror, change the port._

An optical switch routes light from an input fiber to an output fiber without ever turning the signal into electricity. The common form used in data centers is a 3D MEMS optical circuit switch, which is essentially an array of tiny tilting mirrors. A light beam comes in on a fiber, lands on a micromirror, and the mirror tilts on two axes to reflect that beam onto whichever output fiber you want. The mirror is under a millimeter across, it is steered by tiny electrostatic actuators, and it can repoint in milliseconds. Google builds its Jupiter fabric around exactly this, in the part of the network it called Project Apollo.

The contrast is with a normal electrical packet switch. Take an Arista 7800, which is built on Broadcom Tomahawk silicon running Arista's EOS software. The fiber comes in, a transceiver converts the light to electrical signals, the switch ASIC reads each packet header, decides where it goes, queues it, and a transceiver converts it back to light on the way out. Every packet gets inspected and forwarded individually, which is flexible but burns power on all that optical to electrical to optical conversion and on the per-packet decision making.

The optical switch does none of that. It sets up a physical light path from input to output and leaves it there, so the data stays in the optical domain the whole way through. That makes it far cheaper in power and far higher in raw capacity, but it cannot look at packets or make per-packet routing decisions. You reconfigure it by tilting mirrors to change the topology, not by forwarding traffic flow by flow. That trade is why Google uses optical circuit switches to reshape the fabric between blocks while still using electrical packet switches where it needs real per-packet routing.
